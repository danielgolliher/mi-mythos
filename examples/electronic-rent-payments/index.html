Maximum New York · Legislative Brief

Electronic rent payments for NYC housing vouchers

How a one-section local law forced HRA to retire the paper check — and codified a project HRA was already building.

Bill: Int. No. 0704-A of 2022 · Local Law: LL 64 of 2023 · NYC Admin. Code § 21-145.3
Prime sponsor: Council Member Shaun Abreu (with the Public Advocate, Mr. Williams)
Compiled May 4, 2026

What it does

Local Law 64 of 2023 adds a single new section to the NYC Administrative Code — § 21-145.3 — with one operative sentence:

The department shall provide landlords the option to accept rental assistance payments via an electronic transfer into a bank account.

— NYC Admin. Code § 21-145.3(b), as enacted by LL 64/2023

"The department" is the Department of Social Services / Human Resources Administration (DSS/HRA). The covered universe is "recurring payments made by the department to landlords on behalf of tenants pursuant to programs administered by the department" — in plain English, ongoing CityFHEPS subsidy payments and similar HRA-administered vouchers.

One carve-out: state-funded shelter allowance payments under 18 NYCRR § 352.3 (the cash-assistance shelter allowance) are excluded.

The law creates an option for landlords. HRA must offer EFT; it does not compel landlords to accept it.

Why a paper check is not just a paper check

The country's largest family-shelter operator, Win (formerly Women In Need), described the on-the-ground workflow in written testimony at the same hearing:

Per the current protocol, HRA checks can either be mailed to the landlord or picked up by the family's shelter provider at the City's downtown Manhattan office. Although DSS has indicated that it should take about seven days for checks to be delivered to landlords, it often takes much longer. Since waiting for HRA to mail checks can take weeks and jeopardize a family's new apartment, shelter providers instead often opt to go out of their way to secure the payments in person. Once the provider picks up the checks, they must hand deliver them to landlords.

— Win, written testimony, 12/15/2022

What Win is describing: a shelter caseworker rides downtown, queues at HRA's office, picks up a stack of paper checks, and then delivers them by hand to landlords across the five boroughs — because the alternative (the mail) was too slow to keep families from losing apartments they had already been approved for.

And from a voucher holder, Ethel Brown of Neighbors Together — testifying about her own family in real time:

I have firsthand experience with HRA not paying my rent on time. My family and I just moved to an apartment with City FHEPS voucher in late August of this year. After more than seven years… this month, my landlord asked if the voucher money would come to him on time and we told him yes because that's what we expected. Now our yes became a no because HRA did not send the funds in time for our December rent payment.… This is hurting our credibility with our landlord. It created a mistrust of us and the voucher program and for us it's just another experience of the voucher not working the way it should.

— Ethel Brown, CityFHEPS voucher holder, 12/15/2022

The CurRENT subtext

The most revealing feature of LL 64 is the ten weeks it took DSS to walk back from "may preclude the need for this legislation." First Deputy Commissioner Berry's prepared testimony on 12/15/22 framed the bill as redundant with HRA's own modernization project:

We are also eager for the continued rollout of our new landlord management system current, which is under development and will include a landlord portal that will allow all New York City landlords to opt into electronic funds transfer and to see a record of payments processed… we believe that our ongoing efforts to streamline rental payments for landlords, including full implementation of current, will achieve the goals that we all share and may preclude the need for this legislation.

— Jill Berry, DSS, prepared testimony, 12/15/2022

Five weeks later, at the 1/18/23 General Welfare hearing on broader CityFHEPS reform, HRA's own Chief of Homelessness Prevention — pressed by Chair Ayala on the same modernization — described it in plainly future tense:

Chairperson Ayala

And how soon do you anticipate the system to be…?

Bruce Jordan, HRA Chief of Homelessness Prevention

We're—we're currently experimenting with it at certain stages and in certain type of cases and certain type of functions, and we're doing improvements, you know, every so—few months as we learn things, and we're hoping to have it really fully operational by either the end of this year or the beginning of 2024.

Ayala

Yeah. I think that that's going to be a game changer. You guys, you have the most outdated payment processing system, I think city wide.

1/18/23 General Welfare hearing on Int 0894 (CityFHEPS reform), hearing_894_jan23_FULL.txt lines ~5099–5159.

That is the frame for what LL 64 actually accomplished: it converted an executive-branch initiative whose timeline kept slipping into a permanent administrative-code obligation, at zero new cost. CurRENT can be deprioritized; § 21-145.3(b) cannot.

How the bill was narrowed

Two changes between the original Int. 704 and the enacted Int. 704-A are worth noting:

  • Effective date stretched from 90 days to one year. The original bill set a 90-day clock; the amended version pushed it to 12 months — an explicit accommodation of HRA's CurRENT rollout schedule.
  • Definition narrowed to "recurring" payments only, with an explicit carve-out for state-funded shelter allowance under 18 NYCRR § 352.3.

The second carve-out is more interesting than it looks. 18 NYCRR § 352.3 is the New York State regulation governing rent allowances paid out of the state-funded portion of cash-assistance shelter allowance — the older rental-assistance backbone administered jointly by NYC's HRA and New York State's Office of Temporary and Disability Assistance (OTDA). By design, that money flows through state statute and state rule, not city statute. A NYC local law cannot dictate how OTDA delivers its share of a payment. So the Council took the half of HRA's landlord payment universe that is entirely city-discretionary — CityFHEPS, the city-only voucher — and left the state-funded shelter allowance to Albany.

That carve-out is the canary that this is not, fundamentally, a single-jurisdiction problem. The state side of the same rent payment is also paid by mail.

The pattern: Adams vs. Council on rental assistance

LL 64 reads as one bill in isolation. Read across the four-year arc of CityFHEPS legislation, it is the second of four Council interventions over Mayor Adams's stated objection. The escalation in Adams's response is itself the story:

LL 102/2023Int 894-A — CityFHEPS reform package (90-day rule, work requirement, eligibility expansion). Council 42–8 on 5/25/23 · Vetoed by Mayor 6/23/23 · Council overrode 7/13/23.
LL 64/2023Int 704-A — EFT option (this bill). Council 47–0 on 4/27/23 · Returned unsigned by Mayor 5/30/23 · Became law by Charter § 37 default.
LL 145/2023Int 703-A — quarterly timeliness reports. Council 47–0 on 10/5/23 · Returned unsigned by Mayor 11/6/23 · Became law by Charter § 37 default.
LL 176/2025Int 1372-2025 — cap on household rent contribution for voucher recipients. Council 41–7 on 10/9/25 · Vetoed by Mayor 11/7/25 · Council overrode 12/4/25.

The pattern: every CityFHEPS-related bill that reached Mayor Adams was either returned unsigned or vetoed. None were signed. Two of the four were vetoed outright; both were overridden on the merits. The two operational bills that target HRA's plumbing — LL 64 (this one) and LL 145 (the timeliness reports) — got the milder "return unsigned" treatment, which lets the bill become law without putting the Mayor's name on it. The two bills that expand the program (LL 102) or constrain its cost-sharing (LL 176) drew full vetoes.

That this bill got the lighter response is itself a tell. EFT for landlords doesn't redistribute money or expand who qualifies. It just changes how the city pays. Even an administration that opposed every other rental-assistance reform of the period could not bring itself to veto the one bill whose effect was to convert paper checks into bank deposits.

Where the state fits in

The City Council is not the only legislative body looking at this problem. New York State has its own oversight, its own parallel programs, and its own version of the same fight — on a four-year delay, with a different cast.

The Comptroller's parallel oversight

NYS State Comptroller Thomas P. DiNapoli has audited HRA's CityFHEPS administration twice in the relevant period. Both audits read like state-government echoes of the same operational complaints aired on the Council floor in December 2022 — only with state-auditor specificity attached.

October 31, 2024 audit — Administration of the CityFHEPS Program for Department of Homeless Services Shelter Residents (covering July 2019 through December 2023). Among the on-the-record findings:

  • DSS could only produce payment documentation for 12 of 52 cases auditors requested.
  • In 7 cases, DSS "failed to make timely rental assistance payments to landlords."
  • In one case, DSS continued issuing rental-assistance payments for two months after the tenant had vacated the unit and returned to shelter.
  • HRA paid $1.7 million to landlords who were ineligible for the unit-hold incentive between January 2019 and March 2024.
  • Across 52 approved CityFHEPS households, the average shelter-to-permanent-housing wait was nearly 10 months; one household waited more than three years before being approved.
  • The audit notes that "DSS has not taken any steps to investigate why delays are occurring."
  • DSS "disagreed with several of the recommendations."

January 9, 2026 follow-up audit — Administration of the CityFHEPS Program for Department of Social Services Homebase Clients (covering January 2022 through May 2024; reviewed 75 of approximately 8,000 cases and $671 million in contracts to seven nonprofit Homebase providers). Among the findings:

  • Households placed "in apartments with serious housing-code violations, including mold, infestations and missing window guards."
  • "Nearly half of the case files reviewed were missing required proof of income or eligibility."
  • Landlords appearing on the NYC Public Advocate's Worst Landlord Watchlist "continued to receive rent payments."
  • DSS approved rents above market rates by an average of $525 per month without documentation of the required rent-reasonableness assessment.
  • DSS responded that it has updated its case-management systems and plans to launch new monitoring teams "by mid-2026."

Sources: NYS Office of the State Comptroller press releases, October 31, 2024 and January 9, 2026.

Two notes that connect the audits back to the LL 64 / LL 145 story. First: when LL 145 (the quarterly timeliness reports) and LL 64 (the EFT option) were enacted, the Adams administration's argument was that they imposed an unnecessary reporting and operational burden on top of what HRA was already doing. The Comptroller's findings — that documentation existed for less than a quarter of audited cases, that delays went uninvestigated, that ineligible payments ran into the millions — suggest the prior baseline was not as well-managed as the agency's "may preclude the need for this legislation" framing implied. Second: the audits are the kind of independent verification the Council legislated for, just produced at the state level instead of through DSS's own quarterly reports.

The Feb 18, 2025 joint Senate hearing

Six weeks after the NYC General Welfare Committee's January 2025 oversight session that produced the "1,500 landlords on EFT" number, three NYS Senate standing committees held a joint public hearing in Albany on the same set of issues:

Joint Public Hearing: To assess rental assistance programs and examine the need for expanded residential rental assistance to prevent evictions, homelessness, housing instability and excessive rent burdens.

February 18, 2025 — joint hearing of three NYS Senate standing committees: Social Services (chaired by Senator Roxanne J. Persaud), Housing, Construction & Community Development (chaired by Senator Brian Kavanagh), and Aging (chaired by Senator Cordell Cleare).

The hearing's framing bills were S.72 (the Housing Access Voucher Program), S.4446 (Shelter Arrears Eviction Forestallment), and the SCRIE/DRIE elderly & disability rent-increase exemption programs. Testimony came from AARP New York, the Legal Aid Society (Ellen Davidson testified), New Destiny Housing, NYSAFAH, and others.

This is the state-level analog to the NYC General Welfare hearings: the same operational complaints, the same advocacy organizations (Legal Aid in particular shows up at both), the same year. The relevant difference is that the state hearing focused on expanding the rental-assistance toolkit (HAVP, SAEF), whereas the NYC hearings spent late 2022 and 2023 fighting over the plumbing of an existing toolkit (CityFHEPS payment timing, EFT, Cash Assistance backlogs).

Two state programs that show how this plays out elsewhere

FHEPS (state version, 2021). Distinct from CityFHEPS, the state-level Family Homelessness and Eviction Prevention Supplement was raised to 100% of fair market rent in 2021 legislation championed by Senator Kavanagh and Assemblymember Linda B. Rosenthal. The state's modernization push for FHEPS payment mechanics has tracked NYC's a year or two behind — OTDA has a recipient-side direct-deposit form (4148B-DD) but landlord-side EFT for state-funded shelter allowance is not as far along as HRA's CurRENT rollout.

ERAP (Emergency Rental Assistance Program). The pandemic-era state program, administered by OTDA, distributed roughly $4 billion in rental and utility assistance to landlords on behalf of tenants between 2021 and the program's wind-down. New applications closed January 20, 2023; the OTDA portal stayed open until November 17, 2025. ERAP became a working laboratory for the same payment-mechanics question LL 64 addressed: how a state benefit reaches a landlord, how long it takes, what to do when payments are returned or unclaimed.

HAVP — the state's version of the same fight, on a delay

The Council's Council Member Abreu bill on operational plumbing (LL 64) had a state-level eligibility analog: the Housing Access Voucher Program (HAVP), sponsored by Senator Kavanagh in the Senate (S.72) and Assemblymember Rosenthal in the Assembly. HAVP is a state-administered voucher for households who are homeless or at imminent risk; participants pay no more than 30% of income toward rent.

The state pattern looks much like the NYC pattern, with the same actors playing the same roles a step late:

  • Advocates and legislators sought $250 million for HAVP in the FY 2025–26 state budget.
  • Governor Hochul's enacted budget allocated $50 million — one-fifth of the ask.
  • The program is structured as a four-year pilot, March 1, 2026 through May 1, 2030, jointly administered by NYS Homes and Community Renewal (HCR), NYCHA, and NYC's Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD).
  • Senator Kavanagh announced on February 3, 2026 that he would not seek re-election; the bill survives him as a four-year experiment.

The HAVP launch date — March 1, 2026 — is just two months before this report was compiled. It is too early to evaluate whether the state-level voucher will repeat the operational pathologies the city took years to surface.

Administrative and case law

The legislative story is half the picture. Underneath it sits a layered architecture of administrative rule-making and active litigation — the part of the policy machine that actually defines what HRA does next Monday morning.

City administrative law: 68 RCNY Title 68

The CityFHEPS rules live in Chapter 10 of Title 68 of the Rules of the City of New York (68 RCNY ch. 10). HRA promulgates these rules under authority delegated by City Charter §§ 603 and 1043 and New York Social Services Law §§ 34, 56, 61, 62, 77, and 131-a — meaning the city's rule-making authority for CityFHEPS rests on a foundation of state social-services law. HRA cannot simply rewrite CityFHEPS by city rule; the state statute is the floor.

The relevant rule sections include 68 RCNY §§ 10-05 (maximum monthly rents), 10-09 (adjustments to payment amounts and program termination), and 10-15 (additional provisions). HRA has issued multiple emergency rule amendments since 2021 — most consequentially the January 2023 amendment that raised CityFHEPS maximum rents to the Section 8 standard adopted by NYCHA, and the November 2025 rule formally discontinuing the unit-hold incentive (the same incentive the Comptroller flagged as the source of $1.7 million in incorrect payments).

For purposes of LL 64, HRA has not separately promulgated 68 RCNY rules implementing the EFT-option requirement of NYC Admin. Code § 21-145.3(b). The statute is sufficiently self-executing that the agency has implemented it through the CurRENT landlord portal rather than through rulemaking — consistent with the bill's design as a ratification of a project HRA was already running.

State administrative law: 18 NYCRR Part 352

The state regulation that LL 64 explicitly carves out — 18 NYCRR § 352.3 — sits in a much older regulatory framework. Title 18, Part 352 of the New York Codes, Rules and Regulations governs the cash-assistance shelter allowance: maximum allowances, restricted payments, additional shelter supplements, and the rules for direct payments to landlords on behalf of public-assistance recipients. The Empire Justice Center has formally commented on Part 352 to OTDA, urging modernization of the shelter-allowance schedule that has not been substantially updated for the cash-assistance population in decades.

Practically, this means the state-funded portion of any shelter-allowance landlord payment is governed by OTDA directives (the ADM series) and Part 352 — not by NYC rules. The carve-out in LL 64 is not a drafting nicety; it is a recognition of overlapping jurisdiction. New York's social-services statute layers the city's social-services district inside a state supervisory framework, and the same physical landlord can be receiving two different streams of money under two different rule books.

Case law: Vincent v. Adams and the LL 102 expansion fight

The active litigation in this space is Vincent v. Adams (NY Sup. Ct., NY Cnty.) — the suit by Legal Aid, advocates, and individual plaintiff Marie Vincent seeking to compel the city to implement Adams's vetoed-and-overridden LL 102 of 2023 (the 90-day-rule and eligibility expansion). The case has gone three rounds:

August 1, 2024Trial court ruling for the city. NY Supreme Court found the City Council had exceeded its authority in expanding CityFHEPS — the same legal theory the Adams Law Department had argued in vetoing the bills.
Summer 2025Appellate Division, First Department reversed. A five-judge panel held that the City Council did have legal authority to expand the program and directed the Adams administration to implement the reforms. The Adams Law Department announced it was reviewing further legal options.
March 24, 2026Mamdani appeal to the Court of Appeals. Two months into his administration, Mayor Mamdani's Law Department picked up the same argument Adams's lawyers had made — that "the City Council is not authorized to expand the program" — and appealed to New York's highest court.

The appeal is pending as of this report's compilation date. If Mamdani prevails, LL 102 is unenforceable and the Council's authority to legislate the eligibility side of CityFHEPS is constrained for the foreseeable future. If he loses, the expansion could add roughly 47,000 households to the program (per a 2024 IBO estimate) and push annual program cost above $4 billion by 2030.

None of Vincent's arguments touch LL 64, LL 145, or LL 176. The litigation is squarely about whether the Council can expand eligibility — not whether it can regulate operational mechanics like payment method or reporting. That asymmetry is the legal-architecture reason the operational bills survive untested while the eligibility bill remains in limbo: the operational laws sit on uncontroversial home-rule terrain, while eligibility expansion implicates state-supervised social-services-law authority. LL 64's drafters appear to have understood this when they narrowed the bill to recurring city payments and carved out the state-funded shelter allowance.

Source-of-income discrimination: a parallel enforcement track

One legal-architecture footnote that several witnesses pointed to: source-of-income discrimination by landlords (refusing to rent to voucher holders) is enforced by the NYC Commission on Human Rights under NYC Admin. Code § 8-107(5)(a), which has prohibited such discrimination citywide since a 2008 amendment, expanded again in 2021. Council Member Abreu's pitch for LL 64 explicitly framed faster, more reliable EFT payments as a tool against source-of-income discrimination — on the theory that landlord refusal is partly rational risk-management against an agency they don't trust to pay on time. Whether EFT actually moves the discrimination needle is not yet measurable, but it is an example of how operational reform (LL 64) can be designed to support a separate enforcement statute (Admin Code § 8-107) without amending it.

Why this is interesting

  1. It is a "ratifying" statute. NYC was already building CurRENT before the law was enacted. The Council's role here was not to force a project but to lock it in — to convert an executive-branch initiative into a permanent administrative-code obligation, at zero new cost.
  2. It targets a payment-rail problem, not a benefit-design problem. Most CityFHEPS reform debate is about who qualifies (the 90-day rule, undocumented eligibility, voucher value). LL 64 is about plumbing: the bill assumes the program design is fine and attacks the operational drag that turns a 7-day mail target into multi-week delays.
  3. The fiscal case is in the testimony, not in the FIS. The Council Finance Division's official FIS scored this at $0/$0. The actual fiscal case — the difference between $188/day in shelter and $72/day on a voucher — lives in Win's testimony. If even a small fraction of voucher families avoid an extra week in shelter because EFT cleared faster than a mailed check, the savings dwarf any plausible implementation cost.
  4. The Mayor never signed any rental-assistance bill of this period. Across four bills (LL 102, LL 64, LL 145, LL 176) Adams returned two unsigned and vetoed two; the two vetoes were overridden. Even on this bill — a $0/$0 fiscal score, a unanimous 47–0 Council vote, codifying a project HRA was already running — he would not put his name on it.

Where things stand now

Three years after that "zero electronic payments" exchange in Council Chambers, the picture is more complicated than the bill's sponsors imagined — partly because the agency moved (slowly), partly because the political environment around CityFHEPS has shifted under it, and partly because the operational problem the bill was meant to fix turned out to be only one slice of a larger one.

The EFT rollout: from 9 landlords to 1,500

The single most useful update is empirical. From the 1,500-foot view, LL 64 worked.

Dec 15, 20229 landlords in the CurRENT EFT pilot, by Berry's own admission at the oversight hearing. Approximately zero EFT to private landlords agency-wide.
Nov 23, 2024115 landlords fully on EFT, plus another 800 landlords receiving EFT for at least one tenant (i.e., partially enrolled). DSS reporting figure.
Jan 27, 2025~1,500 landlords on EFT for CityFHEPS payments, per DSS testimony at the General Welfare oversight hearing. 91% of CityFHEPS packages are now submitted through the new "Current" landlord-management system — "up from single digits a year ago."

Source: DSS oversight testimony, NYC Council Committee on General Welfare, 1/27/2025 (citymeetings.nyc).

That is a 167× increase in two years. Landlord enrollment in electronic payments was named a "calendar year 2025 priority" by the agency. The strategy DSS described — start with bigger landlords, then push out to the long tail — explains why the count jumped from 9 to 800-plus in the second year of the rollout: a few large portfolio owners cover thousands of voucher tenants apiece. The CityFHEPS package-submission flow (the upstream end of the workflow Win and Marco Balestri described as backed up six-to-twelve months) is now overwhelmingly digital.

The first-payment loophole

One detail worth flagging: HRA's current public guidance still tells landlords that "your first housing payment will be a paper check, delivered by the tenant's housing specialist." Only subsequent recurring payments can flow through EFT once the landlord opts in. This is consistent with the statute — LL 64's definition is limited to "recurring payments," not initial move-in or arrears payments — but it does mean the most operationally fragile moment in the workflow (the first check, when a family is moving from shelter into an apartment) is still on the old rails.

LL 145 quarterly reports: HRA is publishing them

The companion law — which the same Mayor also returned unsigned — required HRA to publish quarterly reports on the timeliness of CityFHEPS payments, beginning March 1, 2024. HRA has complied. Reports are posted at nyc.gov/site/hra/news/legal-notices-rules and now span 2023 Q4 through (at least) 2025 Q2.

For broader context on how strained HRA's processing pipeline still is: HRA processed just 29% of Cash Assistance applications on time in 2023 — and a Cash Assistance application is the prerequisite for a CityFHEPS voucher. The plumbing problem LL 64 fixed sat downstream of a much bigger plumbing problem upstream.

The bigger CityFHEPS fight has moved past payment plumbing

While LL 64 was being implemented quietly inside HRA, the broader CityFHEPS political fight escalated. By spring 2026, three things are true:

  • The program is roughly $1.8 billion a year and serves ~68,000 households. (CityFHEPS spending was $25 million in FY2019.) The Citizens Budget Commission has called this "CityFHEPS Hits $1 Billion: NYC Cannot Voucher Its Way Out of the Housing Crisis." Per-household spending has risen ~25% (~$16,000 to over $20,000 per year), tracking the program's increased rent ceilings.
  • LL 102/2023 (the eligibility expansion) is in litigation. An appellate court ruled the City must implement the expansion. As of March 24, 2026, Mayor Zohran Mamdani — who succeeded Eric Adams in January 2026 and who as a candidate had promised to drop the City's legal challenge on day one — instead appealed to the New York Court of Appeals, arguing that "the City Council is not authorized to expand the program." Mamdani has cited the city's projected $5.4 billion budget gap as the reason for the reversal.
  • LL 64, LL 145, and LL 176 are settled law and not contested. The two payment-plumbing laws (LL 64 EFT, LL 145 reporting) are operative; LL 176 (the rent-contribution cap, vetoed by Adams in November 2025 and overridden a month later) survived the transition. The fight is over LL 102's expansion of who qualifies, not over the operational reforms.

So the Adams-era posture — oppose every CityFHEPS bill but never veto the operational ones — has carried into a new administration that talks about CityFHEPS very differently in office than it did on the campaign trail. The pattern in this report is no longer just an Adams pattern; it is an executive pattern.

What we still don't know

  • Time-to-landlord, before vs. after. Win's testimony described HRA's mailed-check target as "about seven days, often longer." Council Member Abreu's pitch was that EFT would compress that timeline. There is no public dataset I have seen that pairs pre-LL64 mail times against post-LL64 EFT times for the same workflow.
  • What share of HRA's total private-landlord payments are now EFT vs. paper. 1,500 landlords is a denominator-free number without knowing how many landlords HRA pays in any given month. (At the 12/15/22 hearing, Berry could not answer this when Abreu asked it.)
  • Whether the LL 145 timeliness reports show measurable improvement. The reports are public; their substance is not summarized here. Pulling them and trending the on-time-payment percentage across 2023 Q4 → 2025 Q2 is the obvious next analytical step.
  • Whether HRA changed its first-payment workflow. The first-month-rent paper check is still the official public guidance as of January 2026. Nothing in LL 64 forbids HRA from doing first payments by EFT for already-enrolled landlords; whether the agency does this in practice for repeat landlords is unclear.
